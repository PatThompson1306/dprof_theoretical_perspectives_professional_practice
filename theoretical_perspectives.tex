\section{Research context}
\label{sec:research-context}
This paper defines the research context before selecting the most appropriate methodology and strategy for the literature review which follows. 

Empirical observations, supported by academic and grey literature, \citep{finkelhor2024prevalence,CPAI2025} identify that reported instances of \gls{csa} are increasing, with resultant demand on policing outstripping proactive preventative capabilities.

The gap between demand and capability in both \gls{csa} and other operational areas of policing is acknowledged across the sector, generating political and organisational appetite for technological solutions, \citep{npcc2026policeai}.  Despite this appetite, evidence from both professional and academic sources identifes that policing is structurally complex and requires reform to better adopt technology, \citep{homeoffice2024reform}, with cultural barriers further diminishing the successful use of technological solutions, \citep{thompson2021missed,kassem2025their}.

These challenges are significant, yet they do not negate the potential of emerging technologies to address the identified capability gap.  Advances in \gls{nlp} technologies present an opportunity to deliver preventative safeguarding in the \gls{csa} context, acknowledging that the gap between technical possibility and operational deployment is not primarily technical but organisational.  

This paper explores how to bridge this gap, drawing on four thematic research areas to develop a conceptual framework capable of addressing the overarching research challenge: \textit{How can natural language processing methods be developed and operationalised to identify precursors of contact sex offending against children, to enhance proactive safeguarding opportunities within the existing operational constraints of policing?}

\newpage

The four thematic research areas are as follows: 

\begin{enumerate}
  \item What linguistic precursors of contact sex offending exist in offender-victim conversations?
  \item Which \gls{nlp} methods can beused to identify these precursors and be most effectively realised within policing infrastructure?
  \item What implementation barriers exist within policing that may hinder NLP adoption?
  \item What strategies can be developed to overcome those barriers?
\end{enumerate}

These questions provide the scaffoldinge structure of the literature review described in section \ref{sec:purpose-and-scope-of-the-paper}.

\section{Purpose and scope of the paper}  
\label{sec:purpose-and-scope-of-the-paper}
This research is inherently interdisciplinary, positioned at the intersection of technical and organisational domains. Accordingly, a literature review will require a multi-track approach, with each track addressing a different aspect of the research challenge. Each track corresponds directly to one of the four sub-questions introduced in section \ref{sec:research-context}, providing the evidential foundation from which each question will be addressed.

The insights gathered across these four tracks are subsequently then synthesised using integrative thematic analysis, identifying points of convergence and tension between them and resulting in a conceptual framework which grounds the evolving research questions presented in chapter \ref{ch:synthesis-conceptual-framework-and-research-questions}.

\section{Review Methodology and Rationale}
\label{sec:review-methodology-and-rationale}
This literature review adopts a systematic integrative approach.  \citet{snyder2019literature} documents a number of review types, noting that an integrative review seeks to combine multiple perspectives, synthesise those views, and to develop a new framework or perspective.

The interdisciplinary nature of this research, noted in section \ref{sec:purpose-and-scope-of-the-paper}, necessitates a review methodology that can accommodate multiple perspectives and evidence types.  An integrative design as defined by \citet{torraco2016writing} and \citet{torraco2005writing} allows for a review and synthesis across domains, building a conceptual framework which seeks to unify the disparate domains into a framework for operationalising and implementing an \gls{nlp} solution into a safeguarding \gls{csa} context.

A systematic approach is layered into this integrative approach to allow for a targeted and transparent search and selection process in reviewing the evidence, ensuring that the review is both comprehensive and replicable.  This systematic layer is particularly important given the breadth of this review, which spans technical, organisational and ethical domains, each with their own bodies of literature and evidence types.  \citet{whittemore2005integrative} identify that by adding a systematic approach to an integrative review, findings and subsequent synthesis across domains can lead to the delivery of evidence based practice improvements.

From an academic perspective, a doctoral literature review needs to be defensible and reproducible.  Whilst the integrative approach allows for synthesis and framework development, a systematic approach ensures reproducibility whilst adding a counter balance to my own personal biases and my tendency to approach problems with a solutionist mindset.

\section{The scale of CSA reporting}
\label{sec:the-scale-of-csa-reporting}
The measurement of crime statistics in the \gls{uk} is complex, with two primary sources of data providing different perspectives on crime \citep{ariel2019crime}. The \gls{csew} acts as a household survey capturing participants' self-reported experiences of crime, whilst police recorded crime data captures the volume of offences formally reported to and recorded by police forces. 

Neither dataset can be considered a definitive measure of crime, and this limitation is thrown into sharp relief when examining \gls{csa}.

The \gls{vkpp} reported 122,768 \gls{csa} offences in 2024 \citep{vkpp2025national} taken from police recorded crime data, whilst the 2024 \gls{csew} estimates that 1 million people had experienced sexual abuse in the previous year, \citep{csew2024crime}.  

It is important to note that these two datasets are not directly comparable. The \gls{vkpp} \gls{csa} figure represents criminal offences reported to police forces within a single calendar year. The \gls{csew} figure is a cumulative prevalence estimate drawn from a cross-sectional household survey of adults aged 16 and over recalling experiences.  

These discrepancies in reporting the scale of \gls{csa} offending highlights the challenges in measuring the scale of the issue and the demand it places on policing.  The \gls{vkpp} figure represents the formal demand on policing resources, while the \gls{csew} figure suggests a much larger underlying prevalence of \gls{csa} that may not be fully captured by police recorded crime data.  Additionally, Whilst the described reporting of \gls{csa} focusses on physical offending, \citet{finkelhor2024prevalence} suggests that online sexual abuse and communication, when taken into consideration, would markedly increase the prevalence of \gls{csa}.

This is a view supported by \citet{wefers2024understanding} who leans into figures provided by the \gls{nspcc}.  Whilst the source of \citet{wefers2024understanding}'s \gls{nspcc} data is no longer accessible, more contempory reporting by the \gls{nspcc} suggests an 89\% increase in sexual communication offences between 2018 to 2024, \citep{nspcc2024online}.

Starkly, these \gls{nspcc} figures relate to only 7,000 reports captured by \gls{nspcc} Freedom of Information requests to police forces, \citep{nspcc2024online}.  The research of \citet{finkelhor2024prevalence} and \citet{wefers2024understanding}, when aligned to this low number, suggests that the scale of \gls{csa} associated to online sexual abuse and communication is not being fully captured by police recorded crime data, and that there may be a significant gap between the prevalence of online sexual communications and the demand it places on policing resources.  

\section{The demand on law enforcement}
\label{sec:the-demand-on-law-enforcement}
Both academic and grey literature identify that the demand on policing resources from \gls{csa} is increasing, with the gap between demand and capacity widening.  

\citet{jay2022report} suggests that the scale of known \gls{csa} demand may never be met by law enforcement, identifying that digital forensic delays and a constantly shifting set of policing priorities both impact on law enforcement's ability to tackle the scale of the issue. \citet{casey2025gbcse} adds to this summation, noting complexities around fragmented policing systems and a lack of intelligence sharing further exacerbate the potential to tackle demand.  

Beyond these structural blockers, the challenges operate on two distinct dimensions. \citet{li2020identifying} identifies the first as one of volume and process: the manual review of suspect communications is lengthy and resource-intensive, typically occurring after a contact offence has already taken place. \citet{winters2022universal} identify a second, more fundamental dimension: that law enforcement does not fully understand the linguistic and behavioural complexities inherent in grooming communication itself, limiting the capacity for proactive identification even 
where resource permits.

Together with the prevalence gap identified by \citet{finkelhor2024prevalence} and the \gls{csew}, these dimensions suggest that policing is largely confined to reacting to known demand rather than proactively safeguarding children. This has direct implications for the potential of technological intervention, and raises the question of whether the linguistic complexities of grooming communication are computationally tractable — a question the following sections begin to address.

\section{The grooming to contact offending pathway}
\label{sec:the-grooming-to-contact-offending-pathway}
The academic literature which describes the process of grooming a child before the commission of a sexual offence is expansive and complex.  Three contemporary grooming models have been identified from the literature;

\citet{winters2017stages} provide a five stage \gls{sgm}.  This model has been subject to review and validation, \citep{winters2020validation} and has led towards the consideration by \citet{winters2022universal} of a universal definition of grooming.  The \gls{sgm} however focusses exclusively on in person grooming pathways, and does not offer insight into how grooming may occur in an online or digital context.  

\citet{lorenzo-dus2016understanding} provides a staged model of grooming focussing on the online context only.  This model, entitled the \gls{ogdm}, has been expanded over time using growing corpora of conversational data, \citep{lorenzo-dus2017cause,lorenzo-dus2019your,lorenzo-dus2020communicative} and has been considered in operational law enforcement settings, \citet{swansea2023dragon}.

Whilst the models presented by \citet{winters2017stages} and \citet{lorenzo-dus2016understanding} are practical in nature, \citet{elliott2017selfregulation} presents a more theoretical model of grooming, describing the process as a self-regulatory process which is influenced by both an offender's psychological make up in addition to an ongoing external feedback loop affecting external behaviours.  This model is less practical in nature but provides a structured theoretical framework through which to understand the grooming process.

\section{Synthesising the existing grooming models}
\label{sec:synthesising-the-existing-grooming-models}
The models presented in table \ref{tab:grooming-models} all identify that the grooming process is patterned, staged and, in principle, identifiable (albeit with mixed success, \citep{winters2016knew, winters2017stages}).  

Each of the models offers are supported by degrees of rigor, with the \gls{sgm} being empirically validated by experts in the \gls{csa} field, the \gls{ogdm} leaning into the computational linguistics and grounding itself in Speech Act Theory, \citet{lorenzo-dus2016understanding, lorenzo-dus2017cause}, and the \gls{srmsg}, presenting as a theoretical model grounded in Control Theory, \citet{elliott2017selfregulation}.

Whilst these models advance scholarly understanding of the grooming process, they are incommensurable.  The \gls{sgm} concerns itself entirely with in-person grooming and, whilst validated by experts, is based on fictional "vignettes" and not on real world data.  The \gls{ogdm} focusses on the online grooming context only, using chat data captured from the well known open source data set presented by the \gls{pj} Foundation; http://www.pjfi.org/.  This data set, collected between 2004 and 2016 presents adult-decoy interactions with online offenders, potentially missing the nuance and language that may be present between an adult groomer and a child victim.  The \gls{srmsg} is a theoretical model which synthesises previous models and is grounded in Control Theory, but it does not offer practical insights into the grooming process and is not based on real world data.

The difficulty in synthesising these models can be seen in the effort by \citet{winters2022universal} who attempt to produce a universal definition of sexual grooming.  They produce a definition based on a systematic review of thirteen preceding definitions, however their concluded definition text is grounded in their \gls{sgm} and so is inherently biased towards in-person grooming. 

None of the presented models leverage real operational data taken from a law enforcement context, and whilst all of the models define a staged process of grooming, none of them are able to apply real-world insight into how contact sexual offences manifest from the grooming conversation.

\section{The case for technological intervention - Identifying the research opportunity}
\label{sec:the-case-for-technological-intervention}
The work of \citet{winters2016knew,winters2017stages} identify the difficulties inherent in human identification of grooming content.  These observations are compounded by \citet{li2020identifying} who notes the lengthy periods that are spent by law enforcement in reviewing suspect chat logs, often in circumstances after a contact offence as occured.

Together, these challenges present a compelling case for technological intervention. Chapter \ref{ch:nlp-and-computational-approaches-to-grooming-detection} evaluates the technical landscape of \gls{nlp} in the \gls{csa} context, assessing the current landscape of computational approaches to grooming detection.

\chapter{NLP and Computational Approaches to Grooming Detection}  
\label{ch:nlp-and-computational-approaches-to-grooming-detection}
\section{Natural language processing. A technical baseline}
\label{sec:natural-language-processing-a-technical-baseline}
% Transformer architecture as the technical baseline; 2018 as the inflection point 
\gls{nlp} is defined by \citet{josegonzalez-gomez2024analyzing} as a subset of \gls{ai} which \textit{works with text processing capabilities to understand texts like humans do}.

Amongst the many use cases which can be applied to \gls{nlp} technologies, the concept of text classification is relevance to this research.  Text classification is an established \gls{ml} methodology whereby algorithms are trained on labelled data to categorise text into predefined classes.  The trained algorithm can then be applied to new, unseen text to predict the appropriate category based on learned patterns and features, \citep{belcic2024what,josegonzalez-gomez2024analyzing}.

The technical landscape of \gls{nlp} has been transformed by the development of transformer architecture, \citep{vaswani2017attention}, which 
enables a parallel computational processing approach as oppsoed to a sequential one,capturing contextual relationships between words and providing the foundation for \gls{llm} technologies capable of \textit{understanding} language at scale, \citep{khanna2024evolution, raiaan2024review}.

\section{Classification in the grooming context}
\label{sec:classification-in-the-grooming-context}
As early as 2012, computer scientists were exploring the application of \gls{ml} and \gls{nlp} techniques to the detection of grooming content in online conversations, \citep{inches2012overview}.  Since then, there has been a growing body of technical research in this area, with a predominent focus on classifying text content as either being indicative of \text{grooming} or \text{not grooming}.

\citet{leiva-bianchi2025effectiveness} provides a recent systematic review of the published literature which tackles the grooming classification problem identifying relevant \gls{ml} algorithms used, the datasets which fed that classification process and the effectiveness of those algorithms.  A summary of of \citet{leiva-bianchi2025effectiveness}'s work is presented in table \ref{tab:nlp-studies-summary}.  It is immeciately apparent that the \gls{pj} dataset, and derivatives of it, noted as PAN12 or PAN13, are the predominant data source for this research, with a significant number of studies using these datasets to train and test their algorithms.  The \gls{pj} dataset is noted in section \ref{sec:synthesising-the-existing-grooming-models} as being a dataset of adult-decoy interactions with online offenders.  Further considerations around this dataset are explored in section \ref{sec:the-data-provenance-challenge}.

The predominant goal across the studies shown in table \ref{tab:nlp-studies-summary} was to provide accurate classification as to whether a given message or conversation could be classified as being indicative of grooming or not.  It is notable from the commentary provided by \citet{leiva-bianchi2025effectiveness} that whilst the studies rightly concern themselves with the accuracy of classification, there is a lack of focus in grounding any accuracy metric against any theoretical grooming models such as those described in section \ref{sec:synthesising-the-existing-grooming-models}.  

Some studies not captured by \citet{leiva-bianchi2025effectiveness} do seek to provide a criminological or psychological grounding to their classification approaches;

\citet{hamm2025comparing} ground their grooming detection methodology in Social Exchange Theory, noting that the grooming process is one of social transactional exchange between the offender and the victim.  \citet{hamm2025comparing} use this theoretical grounding to develop a model in which positive and negative sentiment identified from message strings are used as part of the classification process. 

\citet{an2025revisiting} use \gls{lct} to frame their classification approach.  \gls{lct} is a grooming framework proposed by \citet{olson2007entrapping} which ientifies a five part cyclical grooming process, shown in figure \ref{fig:luring_communication_theory}.  \cite{an2025revisiting} use \gls{lct} as the labelling basis for their classification training process.

Whilst the papers considered in table \ref{tab:nlp-studies-summary} and noted above focus predominantly on the technical aspects of grooming detection, \citet{lorenzo-dus2019your} and \citet{lorenzo-dus2020communicative} provide a more linguistically grounded approach. Whilst neither paper provides a technical classification model, both employ technical analysis of linguistic features using methodologies such as \gls{cads} and \gls{lwic}, with \citet{lorenzo-dus2016understanding,lorenzo-dus2017cause,lorenzo-dus2020communicative} grounding their analysis firmly in established linguistic theory.

Cumulatively however, the literature predominantly focuses on binary language classification (grooming vs. non-grooming), often without clear grounding in a criminological or psychological grooming model.

Treating grooming detection as a binary classification problem reduces the grooming process to a discrete state rather than recognising it as the dynamic, escalating process described in the literature. Section \ref{sec:the-grooming-to-contact-offending-pathway} describes the \gls{sgm} and \gls{ogdm} models, both of which note a sequential progression through distinct grooming stages. This multi-stage process is similarly reflected in \citet{olson2007entrapping}'s \gls{lct} model, shown in figure \ref{fig:luring_communication_theory}.

The escalatory and sequential nature of the grooming process suggests that linguistic markers indicative of escalation toward contact offending may be identifiable within 
conversational data. This identification of this threshold, the point at which an online grooming offence transitions toward physical contact, remains underexplored in the existing literature, representing a significant gap with direct relevance to the demand concerns across operational proactive safeguarding, noted in section \ref{sec:the-demand-on-law-enforcement}.

\section{The data provenance challenge}
\label{sec:the-data-provenance-challenge}
Table \ref{tab:nlp-studies-summary} clearly shows the predominant use of the \gls{pj} dataset across research in this area.

The \gls{pj} dataset, and derivatives of it (PAN12, PAN13), are the predominant data source for the technical research in this area.  The \gls{pj} dataset is a collection of adult-decoy interactions with online offenders.  Derivatives of this data set appended non-offensive content to the decoy data to create a more comprehensive dataset.  Despite its widespread use, the dataset has significant limitations which are summarised as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The use of adult decoys is described as lacking authenticity and potentially being unrepresentative of real-world grooming conversations, \citet{an2025revisiting,bihani2025evaluating,evans2025corpusassisted}.
    \item The dataset was collected between 2004 and 2016.  It is noted that the decade that has followed has seen new communication patterns and platforms emerge, potentially leaving the language and communication styles in the \gls{pj} dataset as not reflective of current grooming conversations, \citet{agarwal2022predatory,xiao2025adapting,lorenzo-dus2020communicative}.
    \item The original \gls{pj} dataset is limited in size, containing only a small number of grooming conversations.  This lack of diversity in the dataset may limit the effectiveness of any models trained on it, \citet{an2025revisiting,inches2012overview}.
    \item There has been an over reliance on the \gls{pj} dataset across the research community, further limiting the generalisability of any results, \citet{an2025revisiting,leiva-bianchi2025effectiveness}.
 \end{itemize}

 Whilst algorithmic models can be trained on synthetic data, the lack of real world data is a significant barrier to the development of an operationally deployable model.  The language and communication styles in real world grooming conversations may differ significantly from those in the \gls{pj} dataset, and without access to real world data, it is difficult to conceive that any model not trained on real world data can effectively identify grooming content in an operational context.

\section{The operational gap}
\label{sec:the-operational-gap}
Whilst section \ref{sec:the-data-provenance-challenge} identifies the data challenge in classifying grooming content, \citet{an2025revisiting} identifies that efforts to technically classify chat content as "grooming" or "not grooming" have primarily been considered from a post-hoc perspective.  This "after the event" classification mirrors the operational reality noted in section \ref{sec:the-demand-on-law-enforcement} where law enforcement are often in a position of having to review large volumes of chat data after a contact offence has occurred.  The opportunity for proactive safeguarding, to identify grooming content before it escalates toward contact offending, remains underexplored in the existing literature.  \citet{an2025revisiting} makes the case for attempting to the identify the optimal intervention point in the grooming process, however, as with the majority of the existing literature, their work is based on the \gls{pj} dataset and so is limited by the data provenance challenge noted in section \ref{sec:the-data-provenance-challenge}.

Whilst many of the classification efforts claim success in proving concept in identifying grooming content, those that progress to a potential real world use case lean towards considering detection into social network chat groups or capturing live feeds in online conversations.  \citet{s.e.abdelhamid2026detection} discuss the delivery of a technical solution entitled \textbf{SafeGuard} which theorises the delivery of a grooming detection model which coould be used in the home or in schools, however this does not progress into realisation.  \citet{agarwal2022predatory} and \cite{xiao2025adapting} both contextualise their classification efforts within the realms of instant messaging platfroms, but neither offer real world deployment insight.  One of the possible reasons for this is noted by \citet{street2025enhanced} who notes the prevalence of \gls{e2ee} across modern messaging platfroms making the capture of live data for real world deployment a significant challenge.  

For the law enforcement context specifically, the operational gap remains significant.  Multiple studies identify the potential for grooming classification to have a positive impact on safeguarding practice across law enforcement, \citep{inches2012overview,agarwal2022predatory,hamm2025comparing} but there appears to be a notable absence of research which considers the practicalities of deploying a potential technical solution to grooming classification within a law enforcement context.  This challenge is discussed in chapter \ref{ch:technology-adoption-and-implementation-in-policing}.
